Three Structural Forces Compressing Independent Growers
2
Import Domination
Mexican avocado imports grew from 55 million pounds in 2001 to 2.78 billion pounds in 2023. California's share of the U.S. market fell from 40% to 8%. Individual growers cannot fight this with individual marketing. Collective positioning is the only lever.
Water Cost Escalation
Basin adjudications drove Las Posas groundwater costs to $64.58/AF. Supplemental supply runs $800 to $1,200/AF. A 100-acre operation facing worst-case rates absorbs $200,000 to $360,000 in annual water expense. These costs do not reverse.
Handler Consolidation
Calavo Growers: $529 million in net sales. Mission Produce: $1.23 billion in revenue. A single grower bringing 50,000 pounds to negotiate against half-billion-dollar operations has no leverage. Pooled volume changes that equation.
These forces are compounding simultaneously. The growers producing this region's premium fruit hold the least power in the system that depends on them. WACA was built to correct that imbalance through collective governance, pooled volume, and shared advocacy.

WACA: What We Do for Independent Growers
4
Pooled Packinghouse Negotiation
Millions of pounds at the table instead of thousands. WACA's founding packinghouse arrangement is with Del Rey Avocado. Terms reflect collective volume, not individual exposure.
Coordinated Harvest Timing
Staggered delivery scheduling prevents market gluts during peak harvest windows. Coordinated timing captures premium pricing that uncoordinated growers systematically miss.
Transparent Settlements
Every deduction, every grade distribution, every dollar accounted for in monthly member reports. No opacity on packinghouse deductions or settlement timing.
Water Advocacy with Legal Standing
Individual growers cannot participate meaningfully in basin adjudications. WACA provides coordinated legal representation, compliance support, and legislative engagement as a shared service.
Group Procurement
Consolidated hauling contracts, bulk packaging, coordinated labor scheduling. Per-unit costs fall when volume is aggregated. Savings flow directly to member operations.
Compliance Infrastructure
Centralized food safety reporting, nitrogen management, water monitoring, and regulatory filings. Shared cost, shared infrastructure, individual benefit.
Sunkist (1893), Blue Diamond (1910), Ocean Spray (1930): the cooperative structure is the most battle-tested model in American agriculture for delivering exactly this outcome. WACA is the only entity coordinating avocado, citrus, and water advocacy under a single grower-governed platform in this geography.

Why No One Has Done This Yet
Import competition, water adjudications, and accelerated grower consolidation have only converged to crisis level in the last three to five years. The urgency that makes WACA necessary did not exist at full scale a decade ago. First-mover advantage is real and time-limited.
Fillmore-Piru: Partner, Not Competitor
Founded 1897, the Fillmore-Piru Citrus Association is Ventura County's oldest cooperative. It is a single-crop packing operation with no water advocacy, no avocado coordination, and no integrated service model. WACA views it as a potential packinghouse partner, not a competitive threat.

Water Cuts: Highest Severity
Legal counsel retained for adjudication advocacy. Multi-basin member diversification limits concentration. Legislative coalition building on AB 1466 is already underway. This risk is the reason WACA's water advocacy function exists.
Actively Managed Risks
Key person risk is mitigated by governance design: 5-seat Board, founder's permanent seat, and documented institutional knowledge. Packinghouse risk is managed by non-exclusive strategy: no single handler controls WACA's volume.
Structural Protections
Break-even at 1,185 acres with 2,000 committed provides a structural buffer no other comparable cooperative has entered with. $183K reserve gives management time to respond. Competitive entry is blocked by Capper-Volstead status and first-mover grower relationships.
